%% Abstract
\begin{abstract}
\sloppy
Brain-inspired spatial cognition offers interpretable alternatives to black-box learning methods for autonomous localization. However, existing bio-inspired frameworks remain limited to 2D environments or simplified visual processing, lacking robust 3D spatial representations under challenging conditions. To address these limitations, this paper proposes NeuroLocMap, a brain-inspired 4-DoF localization-mapping framework integrating vestibular-visual complementary fusion with hippocampal-entorhinal spatial representations. The framework comprises four core modules: dual-stream visual processing (ventral for recognition, dorsal for motion), vestibular IMU modeling, 3D grid cells with face-centered cubic lattice for position encoding, and experience-based cognitive mapping for loop closure. Unlike data-driven approaches, our bio-inspired design achieves real-time performance ($\sim$31 FPS on CPU) with zero-shot deployment capability. Experiments on CARLA (Town01/02/10), EuRoC MAV (MH\_01/03), and KITTI sequence 07 demonstrate 38.1\% average RMSE improvement over EKF baselines, validating the framework's effectiveness for autonomous navigation across diverse environments.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Spatial localization and mapping in complex 3D environments is fundamental to autonomous driving, mobile robotics, and intelligent transportation systems~\cite{cadena2016past}. Modern approaches typically combine cameras, IMUs, and sometimes GNSS to achieve robust pose estimation~\cite{saputra2018visual}. Interestingly, biological systems have evolved remarkably efficient solutions to this problem: animals such as bats navigate 3D spaces by integrating visual cues with vestibular self-motion signals, supported by specialized spatial cells including place cells, grid cells, and head-direction cells in the hippocampal-entorhinal system~\cite{jeffery2013navigating,hafting2005microstructure,taube1990head,finkelstein2016three,solstad2008representation}. These neural mechanisms provide inspiration for developing brain-inspired localization frameworks that could offer advantages in interpretability and computational efficiency over traditional optimization-based or learning-based approaches.

However, translating these biological principles into practical robotic systems faces several challenges. First, most existing bio-inspired frameworks operate in 2D, whereas real-world deployments require robust 3D spatial representation. Second, classical visual template matching is sensitive to lighting and viewpoint variations, limiting place recognition performance in challenging environments. Third, a simple yet effective fusion strategy that combines visual odometry with inertial measurements---without relying on computationally expensive filters like EKF---remains under-explored for brain-inspired localization frameworks.

To address these challenges, we present NeuroLocMap, a brain-inspired 4-DoF localization-mapping framework (pose $x$, $y$, $z$, $\psi$) that integrates a dual-stream visual front-end (ventral pathway for scene recognition, dorsal pathway for motion extraction) and a vestibular front-end for IMU processing, with complementary filtering for multi-sensory fusion. The framework employs 3D grid cells and multilayered head direction cells for spatial representation, and a multilayer experience map functioning as hippocampal-like episodic memory for place recognition and drift correction. For ground vehicles operating on relatively flat terrain (e.g., urban roads, indoor floors), the orientation is dominated by yaw angle $\psi$, while pitch and roll variations are typically constrained by vehicle dynamics ($<5^\circ$). Our 4-DoF representation provides efficient localization for such scenarios~\cite{milford2004ratslam,milford2010persistent}.

The contributions of this paper are summarized as follows:
\begin{itemize}
\item We propose a unified brain-inspired architecture that couples dual-stream visual processing with vestibular modeling and hippocampal-entorhinal spatial representations for 4-DoF localization and mapping.
\item We develop cortex-inspired visual processing with explicit operators for visual template creation, increasing template count from 5 (typical in RatSLAM) to 195 (39$\times$ improvement) for finer-grained place discrimination under challenging lighting and viewpoint variations.
\item We design a lightweight complementary fusion strategy for vestibular-visual integration that operates without computationally expensive filters, achieving real-time performance ($\sim$31 FPS on CPU) without requiring training data.
\item We validate the framework on six diverse scenarios (CARLA Town01/02/10, EuRoC MH\_01/03, KITTI~07), achieving an average RMSE improvement of 38.1\% over EKF baselines across all tested environments.
\end{itemize}

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

With the increasing demand for robust and interpretable localization systems in resource-constrained environments, achieving biologically-plausible spatial representations has become essential for autonomous navigation and mobile robotics. This study proposes NeuroLocMap, a brain-inspired 4-DoF localization-mapping framework that integrates computational models of 3D grid cells and multilayered head direction cells with dual-stream visual processing and complementary vestibular-visual fusion. We describe the bio-inspired spatial cell architecture, the dual-stream visual processing strategy inspired by ventral and dorsal pathways, and the lightweight complementary fusion mechanism for multi-sensory integration. Experimental results across six diverse scenarios (CARLA Town01/02/10, KITTI~07, EuRoC MH\_01/03) verify that NeuroLocMap achieves 38.1\% average RMSE improvement over EKF baselines while maintaining real-time performance at $\sim$31~FPS. The framework increases visual template count from $\sim$5~templates (typical in RatSLAM) to 195~templates (39$\times$ improvement), enabling finer-grained place discrimination for robust loop closure detection. Future work will extend the framework to full 6-DoF pose representation, integrate learning-based place recognition modules, and deploy on neuromorphic hardware to progressively realize training-free, interpretable localization systems capable of real-time operation on embedded platforms.
